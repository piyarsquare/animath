§ I

See + and × on the line

The two things you do to numbers — made visible.
C1 {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

On the line you do two things to a number: add it and multiply it. You can see both — adding slides an arrow, multiplying stretches it, and flips it for a negative.

Simple and familiar. But even here the rules aren’t free: they’re pinned by a few quiet demands — which is where rings, fields, and the odd cousin tropical come in.

A number is a way to add and multiply

A number is a rule for combining — two of them.

A complex number isn’t a dot. It’s a rule for combining points of a plane two ways: how to add them, and how to multiply them.

Fix those two rules and you have the number system. Everything else is downstream.

FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

Formally: a set with + and × obeying the field axioms — an abelian group under +, a group under × away from 0, the two tied by distributivity. “What is a complex number” is answered entirely by those two operations on ℝ²; the rest of the subject is consequence. → fields → algebra
L3 {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

What we ask of “multiply”

Two demands — distribute, and have a 1.

To deserve the name, a multiply should:

  • distribute over addition, and
  • have a 1 that changes nothing.

That’s nearly all we ask. The surprise is how much it forces — and how little.

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

Bilinearity (distributes on both sides) plus a unit; take commutative and associative too. On the line these pin multiplication down completely. On the plane they leave exactly one free number. The whole trail is the gap between “completely” and “exactly one.” → L4 → PL
L4 {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

On the line, the demands force everything

One slot, no choices left.

Give a number one coordinate and apply the demands. They force the whole table — even (−1)·(−1) = +1. That sign rule is a theorem, not a convention.

The line leaves no choices. (the plane will leave exactly one.)

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

Every integer is a sum of ±1s, so distributivity over the additive group computes every product; (−1)(−1) = +1 falls out of 0 = (−1)(1 + (−1)). On ℚ, then ℝ (with continuity), the product is the unique one. The only escape is to drop the additive group — to give up subtraction. &rarr ; tropical
AX {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

Why we fix addition

We hold addition still — on purpose.

We hold addition still on purpose. Two reasons:

  • the points should be the plane — they combine like arrows. That’s vector addition.
  • we want to subtract: every move undoable.

Change either and you’ve left. tropical leaves — its “add” is min, which has no undo.

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

A ring fixes the roles. + is an abelian group — commutative, every element invertible, so subtraction exists. × is layered on by distributivity and need not be invertible (0 has no reciprocal). The two operations aren’t interchangeable: + is the rigid base, × the free choice. That asymmetry is why we pin + and vary ×.

Still, fixing + is a choice, resting on two demands: (i) the carrier is the plane as an ℝ-vector space, so + is vector addition; (ii) + is a group, so you can subtract. Drop (ii) and a consistent arithmetic survives with a non-invertible + — a semiring. tropical (min, +) is exactly that, and it reassigns which operation plays the un-undoable role.

+ and × on the plane — now there are choices

In the plane, multiplication is a choice.
C2 {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

Give each number a second coordinate — step into the plane. Addition comes along unchanged. Multiplication does not: there’s more than one way to multiply two points.

Treat the two numbers as strangers

What if the coordinates have nothing to do with each other?

Try the simplest thing: treat the two coordinates as strangers — multiply each on its own.

(a,b) · (c,d) = (a·c, b·d)

A real, working multiplication. Follow where it leads and it turns out to be one particular plane: j² = 1, the split one. → SP

FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

Coordinate-wise product on ℝ² is the ring ℝ × ℝ — two independent number lines. In the basis e± = (1 ± j)/2 it reads j² = 1: idempotents e±² = e±, e₊e₋ = 0, zero divisors and all. “The slots are strangers” is the split plane in disguise. → SP
PL {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

The plane has one knob

Two coordinates, and a single dial — j² = p.

Step to two coordinates. Addition rides along unchanged (AX). Multiplication, under the same demands, collapses to a single number: what j² equals.

Call it p. One dial. Turn it and the whole arithmetic changes.

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

A bilinear unital product on ℝ² is fixed by j·j, where j = (0,1). Complete the square and rescale j and it reads j² = p for a real p. The entire choice of “how to multiply the plane” is that one real parameter. → DV
DV {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

Three planes, at the cuts

p < 0, p = 0, p > 0 — three different worlds.

The rescale you just ran at FIG. 4 is the classification: every p slides to −1, 0, or +1, so the dial holds exactly three planes — one per sign. Their addresses:

  • p < 0  →  complex → CX
  • p = 0  →  dual → DU
  • p > 0  →  split → SP
the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

What the rescale cannot do is cross the cuts: ĵ = j/√|p| divides by a positive number, so the sign of p never changes, and nothing reaches 0. The sign is the invariant — and the three classes are genuinely different rings, not just different coordinates: count their rails, or their zero divisors. → WH

It was a quadratic all along

t² = p — the same count runs through solving quadratics.

t² = p is the whole story in miniature: two roots, one, or none, as p is positive, zero, negative. The rail count, the plane, and “how many solutions does this quadratic have” are one question. → FTA

§ III

The three planes — what the choice gives

Three worlds — spin, shear, squeeze — and one is special.
C3 {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

Each choice of p is a whole world. Three of them — and the same count that gives each world its “rails” decides everything else. Only one of the three lets you always divide.

each panel below opens with its orbit — the dashed lines are the rails, directions multiplication only slides along. count them: 0, 1, 2.

CX {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

The complex plane (p = −1)

Multiply = turn. The plane that spins.

Set j² = −1. Now multiplying by a fixed number turns the plane — and scales it. No direction is left pointing where it started, so there are no real rails. (WH)

It’s the one plane that’s a field: every nonzero number is stretchable, so you can always divide. (FD) It’s also the only place √−1 can live.

This is the plane your analytic functions ride on. → analysis

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

With j² = −1, (a+bj)(c+dj) = (ac − bd) + (ad + bc)j — rotation-and-scale: in polar form moduli multiply and angles add. The norm a² + b² is positive-definite, so the only non-invertible element is 0; that is exactly what makes it a field.

Equivalently, “multiply by a+bj” is the matrix [a −b; b a], a scaled rotation. Its eigenvalues a ± bj are not real, which is the same fact as “no real rail.” See it live at FIG. 6 below. → matrices
P = 0 · SHEAR · 1 RAIL
DU {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

The dual plane (p = 0)

Multiply = shear. ε² = 0.

Set j² = 0 (write it ε). Multiplying shears the plane — one direction holds, everything slides along it.

The slide carries a derivative for free. That’s where automatic differentiation lives. → autodiff

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

(a + bε)(c + dε) = ac + (ad + bc)ε — the ε-part is the product rule, exactly. ε is nilpotent, so the whole ε-axis is non-invertible: not a field. One rail (a single real eigenvalue, repeated). → autodiff
P = +1 · SQUEEZE · 2 RAILS
SP {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

The split plane (p = +1)

Multiply = squeeze. Two rails. = ℝ×ℝ.

Set j² = +1. Multiplying squeezes the plane along a hyperbola — two directions hold their line (two rails), the rest slides between them.

It’s the “strangers” multiply from L2, and the squeeze is a Lorentz boost. → relativity

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

j² = 1 factors: (j−1)(j+1) = 0. In null coordinates it’s ℝ × ℝ, two independent lines — hence zero divisors, no field. The unit set x² − y² = 1 is a hyperbola; multiplying by a unit is a hyperbolic rotation = a boost. → relativity
WH {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

How many rails?

Count the real square roots of p — 2, 1, or 0.

Ask each plane: is there a direction multiplication only slides along, never turns? — a rail. The rail count is the real roots of t² = p:

  • p > 0  →  2 rails (split)
  • p = 0  →  1 (dual)
  • p < 0  →  0 (complex)

One count; three faces. And you can watch the count: let ×w carry the whole grid, and see which lines hold.

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

Rails are real eigenvectors of “multiply by j,” eigenvalues solving t² = p. Two real roots → diagonalizable → ℝ×ℝ; one → a defective shear; none → a genuine rotation with no real invariant line. The same number is the unit-curve type and the orbit shape. → CX → DU → SP

FD {{ s.label }} {{ s.label }}

Only one is a field

Just the complex plane lets you always divide.
↪ from L5 — call a number stretchable if something multiplies it back to 1. On the line: stretchable = not zero.

Among the three, only the complex plane is stretchable through and through: every nonzero number divides. The dual and split planes hold nonzero numbers you can’t undo (zero divisors). So only p < 0 is a field.

the full working ⌄
FULL — TEXTBOOK DEPTH

The norm Nₚ = x² − p·y² is multiplicative in all three. It’s positive-definite only for p < 0 — there the sole non-unit is 0, a field. For p = 0 it’s degenerate (the ε-axis); for p > 0 indefinite (a null cone): both carry zero divisors. Demand a field in the plane and the dial is forced to −1. → higher

Fields
Number systems where you can always divide.
A field is a number system where add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by 0) all behave. ℚ, ℝ, ℂ are fields; the dual and split planes are not.
→ expand later · the page that decides this is FD
The worlds of algebra
Groups, rings, fields — the zoo these planes live in.
Step back and the planes are specimens in a larger collection: groups (one operation), rings (two), fields (two, fully invertible). Naming the genus makes the differences between our three planes ordinary.
→ expand later
Stretchable
A word we’ll lean on.
Call a number stretchable if something multiplies it back to 1 — if you can divide by it. On the line the rule is dead simple: stretchable = not zero.
hold the word — it decides more than it looks. it’s quoted into FD · higher
Tropical — give up subtraction
Let “add” mean min. A world with no undo.
The line’s rules were forced — if you keep subtraction. Drop it.
In tropical arithmetic “add” means take the smaller and “multiply” means ordinary add: 3 ⊕ 5 = 3, 3 ⊗ 5 = 8. Consistent, useful — and there’s no subtracting your way back.
FULL
(ℝ ∪ {∞}, min, +) is a semiring: min has no inverse, so it isn’t a ring. It still distributes, it linearizes hard optimization (shortest paths, scheduling), and it underlies tropical geometry. The lesson: a system’s rules are the price of the guarantees you demand. → AX
Clock arithmetic
Wrap the line at n — sometimes a field, sometimes not.
Add and multiply, then wrap around at n. When n is prime it’s a field; when it isn’t, zero divisors appear (2 · 3 = 0 mod 6) — the same “stretchable or not” question on a finite set.

The p-adic numbers
A different idea of which numbers are “close.”
Keep the arithmetic, change the metric: call two numbers close when their difference is divisible by a high power of a prime. A whole parallel analysis falls out.

The fundamental theorem of algebra
In ℂ, every polynomial has all its roots.
The complex plane is where t² = p always has a solution — and not just quadratics: every degree-n polynomial has exactly n roots there. The plane completes algebra.

Complex numbers are 2×2 matrices
a + bj is the matrix [a −b; b a].
“Multiply by a + bj” is the real matrix [a −b; b a] — a scaled rotation. Multiplication, rotation, and eigenvalues become one picture, and “no real rail” becomes “no real eigenvector.” The matrix is live at FIG. 6.

Complex analysis
Where analytic functions live.
Once multiplication is rotation, differentiable functions become conformal (angle-preserving), and the machinery of a complex-analysis course — Cauchy, residues, analytic continuation — is all downstream of the one dial set to −1.
→ expand later
Automatic differentiation
Dual numbers compute derivatives for free.
Carry ε with ε² = 0 through any computation and the ε-part is the exact derivative. This is how modern autodiff — and much of machine learning — actually works.
→ expand later
Split numbers are spacetime
The squeeze in the split plane is a Lorentz boost.
The split plane’s unit “rotation” slides along a hyperbola. That squeeze — fast direction stretched, slow one shrunk, area kept — is exactly a Lorentz boost. x² − y² is the spacetime interval.
FULL
Split-complex numbers x + jt (j² = 1) carry the 1+1 Minkowski form x² − t²; unit multiplication is a boost whose rapidity is the hyperbolic angle. Rapidities add the way the split “angle” adds — the velocity-addition formula falls out. → SP
Can we go higher?
Stretchable number systems exist only in dim 1, 2, 4, 8.
Keep “every nonzero is stretchable” and climb dimensions: ℝ, ℂ, then (dropping commutativity) the quaternions, then (dropping associativity) the octonions — and it stops. Nothing in dimension 3.

The doubling recipe
ℝ → ℂ → ℍ → 𝕆, one doubling at a time.
A machine builds each system from the last by pairing it with itself — ℝ → ℂ → ℍ → 𝕆 — losing one property at every step (order, then commutativity, then associativity).
