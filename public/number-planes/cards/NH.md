## note

A space is **Hausdorff** if any two distinct points fit in non-overlapping
bubbles. The real line is: two numbers sit some distance apart; take bubbles of
half that. So natural it seems automatic.

Sort the planes up to sameness and it fails. Three points — spin, shear, boost.
{spin} is its own bubble (all `p < 0`), likewise {boost}. But every bubble around
**shear** comes from an interval around `p = 0`, which contains both signs: the
only neighborhood of shear is *everything*. Shear is distinct from its neighbors
— and at zero distance from both. The dial shows it: turn toward 0 and circles
flatten into the line pair. You can fall *onto* the wall; you never fall off it.

## full

The pattern is universal: classify anything by **the sign of a rescalable
invariant** and the moduli space is ℝ collapsed to {−, 0, +} — two open points
and a sticky middle whose only neighborhood is the whole space. Instances:

- the planes by `j² = p` (rescaling `j` moves `p` by squares): spin · **shear** · boost
- conics by discriminant: ellipse · **parabola** · hyperbola
- constant-curvature geometries by `κ` (rescaling length moves `κ` by squares):
  sphere · **flat** · hyperbolic
- fixed points of maps: center · **degenerate** · saddle

The sticky middle is always the degenerate case: measure zero, infinitely
fine-tuned, the boundary both open classes press against — reachable by
degeneration (sphere of radius → ∞ *converges to* the flat plane), never left by
perturbation. Manifold theory bans non-Hausdorff spaces as pathology; in moduli
they are information: the certificate of a knife-edge member.

And the Cayley–Klein table is sticky × sticky: both its axes carry this
three-point space, so its **center — the Galilean plane — is doubly sticky**:
in the closure of all nine geometries. The dual numbers sit at the middle of
everything for a topological reason. [[CK]]
