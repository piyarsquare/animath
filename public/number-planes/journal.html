ENTRY 01 · EARLY

are there other options?

When I think of “the number plane,” I think of complex numbers. That was the first sentence in these pages — and underneath it, the question that would not sit still: are there other options?

Before hunting for options, a sharper look at the thing itself. A dot in the plane isn’t a number, any more than a bead on a wire is. What made the line numbers was never its points — it was the two ways of combining them. An addition. A multiplication. Fix those two rules and everything else follows them around.

So the hunt has a shape now: keep the plane’s points, and ask which rules could govern them.

a number system is the pair of rules, not the set of dots. card L1
later — the question found its final form on the cover: does the number plane have to be complex? let’s find out.
ENTRY 02 · NEXT DAY

the demands, tried where the answer is known

Wrote the demands down before touching the plane, to test them where nothing can go wrong. Of a multiplication: it should distribute over addition, and something should play 1. Addition itself stays fixed — points add like arrows, and subtraction must always undo it.

On the line the demands leave no room at all. Every product is forced — even the sign rule: 0 = (−1)·(1 + (−1)) = (−1) + (−1)(−1), so (−1)(−1) has no choice but +1. A theorem, not a convention.

the mirror, twice, is home — that is all (−1)(−1) = +1 says.

The line is finished before it starts. If any choice exists, it lives upstairs.

ENTRY 03 · LATER THAT WEEK

the strangers experiment

Complex numbers are defined by their addition and their multiplication. But other ways of multiplying a plane are easy to write down — so easy it’s almost suspicious that one of them gets all the fame. Tried the laziest first: keep the coordinates apart, strangers to each other — multiply each slot on its own.

FIG. 2 · A WORKED COMPUTATION, TAPED IN
(a, b) · (c, d)  =  (a·c,  b·d)
(1, 0) · (0, 1)  =  (0, 0)
two honest nonzero numbers, and their product is nothing.

It works — distributes, and (1,1) plays 1. But it feels like two lines taped side by side, not a plane: nothing ever crosses between the slots, and the zero-product pairs sit there like a leak in the boat.

Logged as a disappointment. But the disappointment sharpens the hunt: if lazy multiplications are this easy to invent, what exactly are the constraints — and how many survivors do they leave?

ENTRY 04 · A GOOD DAY

one knob

Ran the demands on the plane properly this time. Write a point as x + y·j, with j the unit of the new direction. Distributivity spends nearly all the freedom at once: every product reduces to real arithmetic plus the one product it cannot reach — j·j.

Whatever j² is declared to be — call it p — the entire multiplication follows. All the freedom of the plane, promised by the line’s finished table, is one real number.

ENTRY 05 · TURNING IT

three worlds, not a continuum

Expected a continuum of arithmetics, one per p. Got three. Stretching j is only a change of unit, and it rescales p by any positive amount — so only the sign of p survives honest bookkeeping. Three signs, three worlds; −1, 0, +1 the clean representatives.

FIG. 4 · THE UNIT “CIRCLE” OF EACH WORLD · x² − p·y² = 1
0 rails turn · 1 rail shears · 2 rails squeeze.

And the two-rail world — the hyperbola riding its crossed asymptotes — is the strangers experiment of entry 03, found again from the inside: tilt to the split plane’s own diagonal basis and its multiplication is exactly slot-by-slot. The disappointment was a world all along. So the trichotomy was never a zoo of exotic rules — it measures one thing: how much the two axes entangle.

ENTRY 06 · AFTER THAT

the quadratic was there all along

The count 2 / 1 / 0 keeps following the sign of p around — rails, and now roots. t² = p is the notebook in one equation: two solutions when p > 0, one doubled at p = 0, and for p < 0 none on the line.

FIG. 5 · t² = p · WHERE THE ROOTS LIVE
when they leave the line they do not vanish — they wait, one floor up.

The missing pair is the door. Forcing every quadratic — every polynomial — to keep its roots is forcing √(negative) to live somewhere, and that somewhere has a name and a sign of its own. That argument deserves an entry to itself, when it’s earned.
