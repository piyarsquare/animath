In your plane: × is a rotation · the unit curve is a circle · there are no rails.

Choose how to multiply the plane

A number is something you can add and multiply. In the plane, one of those is a choice — and the choice is this whole page.

On the number line, everything is settled: + slides, × scales, and there's nothing to decide. Step up to the plane and addition is still forced — points are arrows, and arrows add one way. But multiplication is suddenly a choice. Make it one way and you get the complex numbers and their spirals; make it another and you get the arithmetic behind relativity, or the arithmetic behind automatic differentiation. Remarkably, the entire choice collapses to one knob: what does j = (0, 1) square to?

j² = p   and then   (x₁+y₁j)(x₂+y₂j) = (x₁x₂ + p·y₁y₂) + (x₁y₂ + y₁x₂)j

Turn that knob with the three buttons up top. Only the sign of p matters, so there are exactly three planes — and you're holding one right now:

Perspective · the choice, up close

×  Multiplication is the whole choice

Demand only two reasonable things of multiplication — that it distributes over addition and has a unit 1=(1,0) — and almost everything is forced. The product of two arbitrary points is fixed by one unknown: the value of j·j, where j=(0,1). Call it p. Expand:

(x₁+y₁j)(x₂+y₂j) = x₁x₂ + (x₁y₂+y₁x₂)j + y₁y₂·j² = (x₁x₂ + p·y₁y₂) + (x₁y₂+y₁x₂)j

That single p in the first term is the entire decision. In your plane it is p = −1, which makes “times” behave as a rotation: angles add, lengths multiply. The code is literally that one line:

src/animations/Argand/numberPlanes.ts : 60–64 — the rule that defines the class

// Product (x₁+y₁j)(x₂+y₂j) with j² = p. p = −1 is ordinary complex.
export const mul = (a, b, p) => ({
  x: a.x * b.x + p * a.y * b.y,
  y: a.x * b.y + a.y * b.x,
});

Here is the first appearance of the split you were promised. There is a clean way to read “how does × behave”: ask how many directions it leaves un-turned. That count is zero, in your plane — and it is the through-line of everything else here.

Perspective · the 2·1·0 as a curve

◯  The size each plane respects

Pick your plane and it hands you a notion of size for free — the one quantity multiplication never disturbs. It's the norm

N(z) = x² − p·y²,  and it is multiplicative:  N(z·w) = N(z)·N(w).

Set N(z)=1 and you get that plane's version of “the numbers of size one” — its unit curve, the true generalization of ±1 on the line. In your plane it is a circle (the familiar one, x²+y²=1). Multiplying by a point on that curve is the plane's rigid motion: a rotation that slides points around the circle.

A quiet surprise. The everyday “|z|” you know — distance to the origin — is not plane-neutral. It's the circle's answer. Each plane measures size by its own curve, and in two of the three that “size” can be zero for nonzero points (the curve runs off to infinity). Hold that thought — it returns as zero divisors in .

So the trichotomy you met as a behavior of multiplication is now a shape: circle, line-pair, hyperbola. One closed loop, one degenerate in-between, one open curve with two arms. Two · one · zero again — this time as how many ways the curve runs to infinity.

Perspective · the heart of the matter

━  How many rails?

Here is the question that organizes everything. Fix a number α and look at the map z ↦ α·z. Ask: is there a direction it doesn't turn — a line it only slides points along, stretching or shrinking but never rotating off? Call such a line a rail (a mathematician says eigenvector), and the stretch factor its slide (the eigenvalue).

A rail through the origin has some slope t; the point (1, t) stays on its own line under ×α exactly when t solves the plane's defining equation

t² = p

— and now the whole story is just how many real square roots p has. In your plane: p < 0, so t²=p has no real solution — zero rails.

Boost (split, p>0)two rails — separable. The 2-D map is secretly two independent 1-D maps, once you tilt your axes onto the rails.
Shear (dual, p=0)one rail — the borderline. A single slide-direction, with a shear leaning on it.
Spin (complex, p<0)zero rails — entangled. No real direction survives un-turned; x and y can't be pulled apart.

This is the felt core of the whole subject, and it deserves the plainest possible words:

Separable vs. entangled. A separable plane (split) is a disguise — underneath, it's two ordinary number lines running in parallel, and multiplication just runs each one on its own. An entangled plane (complex) has no such disguise: there is no pair of real axes that decouples it, and that irreducible twisting is exactly what makes complex numbers spiral instead of merely stretch. The dual plane sits on the knife's edge between them.

Notice what just happened: the count you first saw as roots of t²=p, then as arms of a hyperbola, is now rails — and it's about to become the shape of an orbit, and then the structure of the algebra. Same two · one · zero, every time.

Perspective · the 2·1·0 as motion

↻  Iterate, and feel it move

Take an affine map f(z) = α·z + β — the plane's version of y = m·x + b — and apply it again and again. Every such map has one balance point, the fixed point z* = β/(1−α), and the orbit lives out the plane's character around it:

Spin (complex)the orbit spirals into or out of z* — and rides a circle when |α|=1. It can't be reduced to anything simpler; that's the entanglement.
Shear (dual)the orbit shears — it drifts steadily along the single rail.
Boost (split)the orbit rides a saddle, sliding along a hyperbola; cover one rail and it's just 1, ½, ¼, …

In your plane the picture is a spiral.

A subtlety worth one sentence. “|α|<1 means the orbit is pulled in” is true on the circle — in the complex plane. In the split plane the same |α|_p<1 can still be a saddle that throws points away along one rail while pulling them in along the other. Size measured by the wrong curve can mislead you; this is the magnitude lens biting back.

Perspective · the 2·1·0 as structure

⊞  Why split is secretly ℝ×ℝ

(Teaser — to be written.) When p=1, j²=1 factors as (j−1)(j+1)=0: two different square roots of one, so the algebra splits. The idempotents e±=(1±j)/2 are a pair of mutually-annihilating “switches”; in the right coordinates multiplication is just (a,b)·(c,d)=(ac,bd) — two independent number lines, exactly the two rails. Complex can't do this (it's a field; x²+1 has no real root), and dual sits between (one repeated root, ℝ[ε]/ε²). The visible scandal: zero divisors — nonzero points whose product is zero, sitting exactly on the null curve.

Perspective · stepping outside the plane

∞  The knob is a circle

(Teaser — to be written.) The dial p looks like a line from −1 through 0 to +1, but it closes up: the dual plane lives at both p=0 and p=∞, the complex arc and the split arc join them into a circle, and swapping the axes x ↔ y is the map p ↦ 1/p (it fixes ±1 and swaps 0 ↔ ∞). Drag p outward and the complex circle flattens into a line — rotation degenerating to the very same shear you'd reach coming the other way.

Perspective · the same split, out in the world

★  Where these planes show up

(Teaser — to be written.) Boost is the arithmetic of special relativity: its “angle” is rapidity, and multiplying by a unit is a Lorentz boost — the hyperbola is the set of equal-proper-time events. Shear is the arithmetic of automatic differentiation: (a+bε) carries a value and its derivative, and ε²=0 is exactly “drop higher-order terms.” Spin is everywhere a rotation is. The trichotomy itself is the split between elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic geometry.

Perspective · a one-way trapdoor

◆  Can I go higher?

(Teaser — to be written.) If a plane is two numbers with a multiplication, can we do three? Hamilton tried for years to multiply triples while keeping magnitudes multiplicative — and it cannot be done. The escape, carved into Broom Bridge in 1843, is to give up commutativity and add a fourth dimension: the quaternions, i²=j²=k²=ijk=−1, whose unit sphere S³ rotates 3-D space. And then Hurwitz closes the door: a magnitude-respecting multiplication exists only in dimensions 1, 2, 4, 8 (ℝ, ℂ, ℍ, 𝕆) — and nowhere else, ever.

This is a leaf — the page's one trapdoor out of the plane. There's a whole world below it (the 4-D complex viewer is one window in), but the trichotomy you came for lives up here, in two dimensions.
