Two planes, one map

The Plane Transform viewer — f shown not as a 4-D graph but as a flat transformation of the colored plane

The other guides build the four-dimensional graph of (z, f(z)) and work to flatten it. Plane Transform takes the opposite, older route: it shows the function as a transformation of the plane — two pictures side by side, the input on the left and where f sends it on the right. There is no extra dimension to project; you simply watch the colored plane get rotated, stretched, folded, and torn. It is the most direct way to feel what a complex function does, and it surfaces one idea the 4-D view hides: conformality.

The applets are the live two-pane viewer (#/embed/plane-transform); the left pane is the input, the right pane its image under f. Drag to pan, scroll to zoom.

1The two panes: same color, same point

The input pane is a colored field of sample points z = x + iy: hue is the argument of z, brightness its magnitude — the same domain coloring as everywhere else (see the color guide). The output pane plots the same points at their images w = f(z), carrying each point's color with it. So the rule for reading the pair is simply:

Same color = same point.   Find a patch of color on the left to know which input it is; find that color on the right to see where f sent it.

Because the color is glued to the point, every move the function makes is visible: a rotation spins the colors, a stretch spreads them, a fold brings two colors together, a zero pulls a whole neighborhood into one spot. 2The same function, shown flat

This is not a different mathematics from the particle viewer — it is the same f, drawn with one fewer dimension. Plane Transform evaluates each output point by the same complex-function library documented in What the functions do; it just plots the result directly in 2-D instead of lifting (z, f(z)) into 4-D first. Conceptually, the output pane is exactly the Drop X + Drop Y shadow of the 4-D graph — keep the output coordinates (u, v), discard the input ones — while the input pane is the (x, y) shadow. Two orthographic slices of the same object, placed side by side so you can watch one become the other.

The trade-off is what each view shows best. The 4-D viewer keeps input and output together, so it can show a continuous surface, its sheets, and its self-intersections. Plane Transform separates them, which loses the surface but wins something the graph obscures: a clean, undistorted look at how the map warps the plane — and, in particular, at the angles between curves.

3Conformality: the signature of differentiability

Here is the payoff. A function that is complex-differentiable (analytic) is conformal: it preserves angles. Two curves that cross at a right angle in the input still cross at a right angle in the output — even where the map bends and stretches them beyond recognition. The grid is the perfect probe: its horizontal and vertical lines meet at 90° everywhere, and under an analytic f the warped grid still meets at 90° in every cell.

Analytic f ⇒ locally a rotation + uniform scale by f′(z). A rotation-and-scale keeps angles — so right angles survive.

That is why the warped pictures, however wild, never shear: locally, multiplication by the complex derivative f′(z) rotates by arg f′ and scales by |f′|, and a rotation-plus-scale cannot turn a square into a non-square parallelogram. Switch on the grid lines in the viewer and you can watch this directly: the cells of z² swell and curve, but each little cell stays a square. The exception proves the rule. Conformality needs f′(z) ≠ 0, and z² has f′ = 2z = 0 at the origin. There, the angle-preservation breaks down and angles instead double — which is why the grid lines fan out into a corner where four cells used to meet. Spotting where the grid stops being locally square is spotting the zeros of the derivative.

4The log-polar plane: turning curves into lines

Plane Transform has one more trick the particle viewer doesn't: it can unroll the plane. The Log-polar view plots each point not at (x, y) but at (arg, log|·|) — angle across, log-radius up. This re-coordinatization is chosen to straighten the most common complex operations into the simplest possible motions:

OperationOn the log-polar plane becomes
multiply by a constant ca translation (by (arg c, log|c|))
zna linear shear / scale (angle ×n, height ×n)
ln zflattens the strip into a square (it is the inverse coordinate map)

The reason is the same Euler identity that powers ez: log(reiθ) = log r + iθ turns multiplication (which adds logs and adds angles) into vector addition. So on the log-polar plane, the rich nonlinear behavior of powers and products collapses into translations and shears you can read at a glance — and combining a polar sampling grid with the log-polar plane turns the input into a clean square lattice, making those shears especially stark.

In the app. The Grid (Cartesian / Polar) and Plane (Standard / Log-polar) toggles live on the viewer's View panel, not on the embed URL. Open the full viewer, set Plane to Log-polar, and switch between z² and z³ to watch the shear angle change with the exponent.
