1.  The line

Number Planes · the trail begins

It starts on a line

A number is something you can do two things with: add it and multiply it. On the number line both feel completely settled — so settled that the rules seem like facts of nature. They are not. They are forced, and seeing exactly what forces them is the thread we'll pull all the way into the plane, where one of the two rules quietly comes loose.

So before we go anywhere, let's look hard at the line we think we already know.

Addition is arrowheads

Picture each number not as a dot but as an arrow starting at 0: +3 is an arrow three units to the right, −5 is an arrow five units to the left. To add, you lay the arrows tip to tail and read off where the last arrowhead lands.

That's the whole of addition, and notice two things we'll want later. First, you can always undo it: every arrow has an opposite that walks you straight back to 0 — that's what subtraction is. Second, the recipe never mentions which way is “positive.” Arrows just add. Hold onto that: addition won't change at all when we step up to the plane.

Multiplication is scale & flip

Multiplying does something different to an arrow: it scales it, and — for a negative multiplier — flips it. Times 2 doubles the arrow; times ½ halves it; times −1 spins it around to point the other way; times −1.5 flips it and scales it by one and a half.

Why must minus times minus be plus?

Here's the question every student asks and most are simply told to accept: why is (−1) × (−1) = +1? Couldn't we just decide that two negatives make a negative? It turns out we can't — not without breaking something we're quietly relying on. The sign rule is a theorem, not a convention.

The thing we're relying on is the one rule that ties addition and multiplication together: distributivity.

Definition Distributivity — the law that lets you multiply across a sum:

a · (b + c) = a·b + a·c. It is the only bridge between the two operations; everything below rides on it.

Watch it force the sign rule, with nothing else up our sleeve but “1 + (−1) = 0” and “anything times 0 is 0”:

  0 = (−1) · 0   ← anything × 0 is 0
    = (−1) · (1 + (−1))   ← because 1 + (−1) = 0
    = (−1)·1 + (−1)·(−1)   ← distribute
    = −1 + (−1)(−1)

  so  (−1)(−1) = +1.  It could not have been otherwise.

Read it again and feel where the freedom went: the moment you insist multiplication distributes over addition, the sign rule is decided for you. In fact on the whole numbers this pins down multiplication entirely — every integer is a pile of +1's and −1's, and distributing across the pile computes every product. The line leaves you no choices. (Spoiler: the plane will leave you exactly one.)

⤷ A path off the trail

Could we ever choose otherwise? — the tropical numbers

We said the rules are forced. The honest fine print is: forced once you keep subtraction. The whole derivation leaned on undoing addition (“1 + (−1) = 0”). Give that up and a different, perfectly consistent arithmetic appears.

In tropical arithmetic, “add” means take the smaller and “multiply” means ordinary add: 3 ⊕ 5 = min(3,5) = 3 and 3 ⊗ 5 = 3 + 5 = 8. It still distributes, it's still useful (it linearizes hard optimization problems) — it just has no subtraction, so none of our sign reasoning applies.

We'll plant a marker here and walk on. The lesson to carry: the rules of a number system are the price of the guarantees you demand of it.

One more habit of the line: everything (but zero) is stretchable

Multiplication on the line has a quiet superpower. Multiply by any number except 0 and you can always get back: there's a reciprocal waiting to stretch you to exactly 1 (3 has ⅓, −½ has −2). Only 0 traps you — nothing multiplies it back to 1.

Let's call a number stretchable when it has such a partner — a multiplicative inverse. On the line the rule is dead simple: stretchable = not zero. It feels like a law of arithmetic itself. Remember the word. Far down the trail, when we ask how high these number systems can climb, stretchable turns out to decide the answer — and the simple rule we just stated is the first thing to crack.

The line, named once

Put it together: on the real line you can add and undo adding, multiply and undo multiplying (except by zero), and the two cooperate through distributivity. A system with all of that has a name — we'll use it once and then mostly leave it in the box where definitions live.

Definition (we'll rarely need it again) Field — a number system where you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide by anything except 0, all obeying the familiar laws (distributivity included).

The real numbers ℝ are the field we grew up inside. The point of everything ahead is that ℝ is not the only one — and that some of its closest relatives are not fields at all.

So the line is closed: addition forced, multiplication forced, no decisions left to make. That is exactly why the next step is a jolt. We give each number a second coordinate — we step into the plane — and addition comes along completely unchanged. But multiplication, for the first time, opens a choice.
